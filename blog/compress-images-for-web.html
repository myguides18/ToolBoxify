How to Compress Images for Web Without Losing Quality (2026 Guide)

Last month a client emailed me a 23 MB "optimized" hero image for their homepage. It was a JPEG exported from Photoshop at quality 100 with dimensions set to 5400×3600 pixels. The slot it was going into on their site? 800 pixels wide. That image never needed to be 23 MB — or even 1 MB. It just needed to be exported correctly.

This happens constantly. People conflate "high quality" with "large file" and they confuse "compression" with "degradation." In reality, a well-compressed image at quality 80 looks identical to an uncompressed one at any normal screen distance — but loads in a fraction of the time. Here's how to find that sweet spot, every time.

The Numbers That Actually Matter

Before you touch a compressor, know your target. I use three thresholds when optimizing images for the web:

  • Hero images (1200–2560 px wide): 200–500 KB. Anything over 800 KB on a hero image will almost certainly hurt your Largest Contentful Paint score.
  • In-content images (800–1200 px wide): 80–200 KB. Blog post images, product grid thumbnails, portfolio layouts — these need to be light because there are usually many of them on a page.
  • Thumbnails (150–400 px): Under 30 KB. A 150×150 thumbnail at 15 KB is perfectly crisp. The same thumbnail at 150 KB is 10× the size for zero perceptible gain.

I arrived at these numbers after benchmarking about 200 pages across different niches (e-commerce, blog, portfolio, SaaS). Pages that kept images within these ranges consistently scored "Good" on Core Web Vitals. Pages that didn't — well, you know the story.

Resize First, Compress Second

This is the #1 mistake I see, and it's the easiest to fix. Resizing a 4800×3200 px photo to 1200×800 px before compression cuts file size by roughly 90% without touching quality at all. A 4800×3200 JPEG at full resolution might be 7 MB from the camera. Downscaled to 1200×800 at the same quality level, it's about 380 KB. Then compress to quality 80 and you're at 95 KB.

Total file size went from 7,000 KB to 95 KB — a 98.6% reduction — and on a 24-inch 1080p monitor, you'd need to zoom to 400% before noticing any pixelation. Nobody does that.

Most online compressors let you set a maximum dimension. I default to 1600 px on the longest edge for general web use. That covers Retina displays (2× pixel density at 800 px display width) with room to spare.

Quality 80 Is the Universal Sweet Spot

I've run blind comparison tests with colleagues — JPEG at quality 80 versus quality 100, side by side, same image. In about 30 trials across five people, nobody could reliably tell which was which at normal viewing distance. The file size difference, though? The quality 100 version was 3–5× larger.

Quality 80 removes about 85–90% of the "invisible" data while keeping the visible structure intact. JPEG compression works by discarding high-frequency color variation that the human eye's rods (brightness sensors, which are more sensitive) and cones (color sensors, which are less sensitive) process differently. At quality 80, you're mostly throwing away color detail your eyes can't resolve anyway.

Quality 70 is where you start to see "blocking" artifacts in smooth gradients (skies, skin tones). Quality 60 is visibly degraded. Quality 90+ is for archival — overkill for the web.

Format Decisions That Precede Compression

You can compress a PNG as aggressively as you want — it's still going to be 3–8× larger than the same image as JPEG at quality 80. The format choice is something you make before compression, and it has a bigger impact than any quality slider:

  • Photographs: WebP (lossy, quality 80) for web delivery. JPEG quality 85 as a universal fallback. A 2400×1600 photo I tested: JPEG q85 = 312 KB, WebP q80 = 168 KB — 46% smaller.
  • Screenshots with text: PNG. JPEG's compression algorithm creates "ringing" artifacts around high-contrast edges — text against a background. It makes reading uncomfortable. PNG keeps every pixel exact.
  • Logos and icons: SVG if possible, PNG with transparency if not. Never JPEG — the artifacts around curved edges and text will make your brand look unpolished.
  • Illustrations with flat colors: PNG-8 if the palette is under 256 colors. Otherwise WebP lossless.

How to Test If You Over-Compressed

Open the compressed image in your browser at the actual display size (not zoomed in). Look at three things:

  1. Smooth gradients — skies, skin, soft shadows. If you see banding (distinct color steps instead of smooth transitions), bump quality by 5–10 points.
  2. Fine text — any overlaid text, labels on charts, UI elements. If the edges look fuzzy, the format is wrong (probably JPEG when it should be PNG).
  3. High-contrast edges — the border between a dark object and a light background. If there's a visible "halo" or noise around the edge, ease up on compression.

One trick I use: compress at quality 70, then at 80, then at 90. Open all three in separate tabs and flip between them. Pick the lowest quality where you can't see the difference. That's your number for that type of image.
