PNG vs. JPEG: The Real Difference

Before converting, understand what you are trading. PNG is a lossless format: every pixel is stored exactly as authored, including an alpha channel for transparency. That fidelity comes at a cost — PNG files are typically 3–10 times larger than a visually equivalent JPEG. JPEG is lossy: it discards data the human eye is unlikely to notice, using a compression algorithm tuned for photographs and gradients. The result is dramatically smaller files, but you permanently lose the original pixel data. Converting PNG to JPEG makes sense when file size matters more than pixel-perfect fidelity and when the image does not require transparency.

When to Convert PNG to JPEG

  • Website images: JPEG loads faster, consumes less bandwidth, and improves Core Web Vitals scores. A hero image at 200 KB instead of 1.2 MB can mean the difference between a passing and failing Largest Contentful Paint metric.
  • Email attachments: Most email clients display images inline, and a 5 MB PNG will either be blocked or take forever to load. JPEG at 80% quality looks identical in an email preview and is one-tenth the size.
  • E-commerce product photos: Platforms like Shopify, Amazon, and Etsy accept PNG but strongly recommend JPEG for faster page loads. A product grid with 20 images loads noticeably faster when all images are JPEG.
  • Social media: Instagram, Facebook, and X (Twitter) all recompress uploaded images anyway. Starting with a well-compressed JPEG gives you more control over the final output than letting the platform's compressor guess.
  • Document embedding: Word documents, PDFs, and presentations balloon when they contain PNG images. Converting to JPEG before embedding keeps files manageable.

When NOT to Convert

  • Logos and icons: These have sharp edges and flat color areas. JPEG introduces compression artifacts (ringing, blockiness) around edges that make logos look unprofessional. Keep logos as PNG or SVG.
  • Screenshots with text: JPEG compression smears text. Small UI labels, code snippets, and terminal output become blurry. PNG preserves text clarity.
  • Images requiring transparency: JPEG does not support transparency. If your PNG has a transparent background, converting to JPEG will fill it with white (or whatever default background the converter uses).
  • Infographics and charts: Similar to logos — sharp lines, text labels, and flat color blocks suffer visibly under JPEG compression.

Step-by-Step: Convert PNG to JPEG Online

  1. Open a browser-based converter. Choose one that processes files locally (no upload to a server). ToolBoxify's image converter handles PNG-to-JPEG entirely client-side.
  2. Drop in your PNG file. Most tools accept drag-and-drop. The converter reads the PNG, decompresses it to a raw pixel buffer, then re-encodes it as JPEG.
  3. Choose a quality setting. This is the most important step. Quality 90–100%: near-lossless, file size reduction is modest (20–30%). Quality 75–85%: the sweet spot — visually indistinguishable from the original for most photos, with 70–90% file size reduction. Quality 50–65%: visible softening, small file, good for thumbnails and previews.
  4. Check the transparency handling. If your PNG has transparency, the converter will replace it with a solid background — usually white. Some tools let you pick the background color. Make sure this looks acceptable before finalizing.
  5. Download the JPEG and compare. Open the original PNG and the new JPEG side by side. Zoom to 100% and check areas with fine detail. If you see artifacts, re-convert at a higher quality setting.

What Happens to Transparency?

PNG's alpha channel is the biggest casualty in a PNG-to-JPEG conversion. JPEG does not store transparency data. When you convert, the transparent areas become solid — usually white, sometimes black, depending on the converter. If your image depends on transparency (a logo on a colored background, a product photo cutout), you have two options: keep it as PNG, or convert to WebP instead (WebP supports both lossy compression and transparency). Check the background color before batch-converting; a batch of product photos with white backgrounds will look fine, but a batch of logos with transparent backgrounds will look broken.

Downsizing During Conversion

Many online converters let you resize the image during the PNG-to-JPEG conversion — and you should take advantage of this. If your PNG is 4000×3000 pixels but the JPEG will only ever be displayed at 1200 pixels wide, resize it during conversion. This combines two operations into one step and produces a much smaller file than converting first at full resolution and then resizing the JPEG later. As a rule of thumb: never serve an image at a higher resolution than your layout's maximum display width.

Common Mistakes

  • Using quality 100 expecting lossless results. JPEG at quality 100 is still lossy — it just discards less data. The file will be large and you have still lost the original PNG pixel data. For true lossless, stick with PNG or use lossless WebP.
  • Converting before checking transparency. Always preview the JPEG with its new background color before sending it to a client or uploading it to a live site.
  • Overwriting the original PNG. Keep the PNG as your master file. JPEG is a delivery format. If you need to edit the image later, you want the lossless source, not a re-compressed JPEG.
  • Using a server-upload converter for sensitive images. Medical images, identity documents, unreleased product designs — these should never leave your device. Use client-side tools exclusively.
