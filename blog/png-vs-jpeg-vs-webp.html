PNG vs JPEG vs WEBP: Which Format Should You Use in 2026?

Here's something I didn't learn until embarrassingly late: the file format you pick matters more than any compression slider. Run a 6 MB PNG photo through the best lossless compressor in the world and you'll get — maybe — a 5.8 MB file. Convert that same photo to JPEG at quality 85 and you're at 400 KB. Convert it to WebP at quality 80 and you're at 220 KB. Same image, same visual quality at screen resolution, 27× smaller. The format is the compression.

After years of picking the wrong format and fixing it later, here's my battle-tested decision tree. No theory — just what actually works, backed by the file sizes I measured on my own machine.

When JPEG Still Makes Sense

JPEG is the cockroach of image formats — it's been around since 1992 and nothing kills it. It still works everywhere: every browser, every email client, every CMS, every printer, every operating system. If you're sending an image to someone and you don't know what they'll open it with, JPEG is the safe choice.

For email attachments specifically, JPEG remains the default because most email clients don't render WebP inline. I learned this when I sent a WebP to a client who uses Outlook 2019 on Windows and got a "file not supported" reply. JPEG at quality 85 is the universal compromise — small enough for email, compatible enough for anything.

When PNG Still Makes Sense

PNG has two things JPEG and lossy WebP can never replicate: perfect pixel fidelity and true lossless compression. Any image where sharp edges and exact colors matter — screenshots with text, technical diagrams, barcodes, QR codes, line art, flat-color illustrations — should be PNG. JPEG's DCT compression creates "ringing" artifacts around high-contrast edges. On a screenshot of a code editor, this manifests as a fuzzy halo around every character. It's subtle at quality 90, obvious at quality 80, and unreadable below quality 70.

The trade-off: PNG files are large. A full-resolution screenshot in PNG can be 4–8 MB. Consider converting screen captures that are mostly photographic content (a UI mockup with a background photo, for example) to lossless WebP instead — you keep the pixel fidelity but get 20–30% smaller files.

WebP: The New Default for the Web

By 2026, WebP has crossed 97% browser support globally. It's the format you should serve on any website because it gives you lossy compression (like JPEG but better), lossless compression (like PNG but better), transparency (like PNG but better), and animation (like GIF but dramatically better) — all in one format. The only reason not to serve WebP is compatibility anxiety, and that's handled by a <picture> fallback.

AVIF: Worth Paying Attention To

AVIF is the format that might eventually replace WebP. In my testing, AVIF produces files 20–30% smaller than WebP at the same visual quality, handles HDR and wide color gamut, and has better compression artifacts (fewer of the blocky JPEG-like artifacts that WebP can produce at very low quality settings). The catch: encoding is 3–5× slower than WebP, browser support is at about 92% (missing some older Safari versions), and CMS support is spotty. For now, WebP is the practical choice for production. Keep an eye on AVIF for 2027–2028.

The Format You Should Never Use on the Web

TIFF, BMP, and raw camera formats (CR2, NEF, ARW) have no place on a website. TIFF files can be 50–100 MB for a single image. BMP is uncompressed and roughly 10× larger than PNG for the same content. Raw files are for editing, not delivery. Always convert these before uploading.

Animated GIFs are the worst offender. They're limited to 256 colors, produce enormous files for even short clips, and have been obsolete since animated WebP arrived.
